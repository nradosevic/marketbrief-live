Tokenized Real Assets: Beyond the Blockchain Hype

By Daniel Okafor • March 9, 2026 • 7 min read

After years of promises and pilot programs, tokenized real-world assets are finally gaining institutional traction. Major financial institutions including JPMorgan, BlackRock, and Franklin Templeton have launched tokenized fund products, while real estate platforms have processed billions in tokenized property transactions. The technology has moved beyond proof-of-concept to genuine commercial deployment, fundamentally changing how certain assets are issued, traded, and settled.

The appeal of tokenization lies in its ability to solve longstanding frictions in traditional finance. Consider private equity: investors typically commit capital that remains locked for seven to ten years, with limited secondary market liquidity. Transfer processes are manual, slow, and expensive. Tokenization enables fractional ownership, 24/7 trading on digital exchanges, and near-instant settlement—transforming illiquid assets into something more closely resembling public securities without requiring public listing.

Real estate has emerged as a particularly active tokenization frontier. Platforms like RealT and Lofty have tokenized thousands of residential properties, allowing investors to purchase fractional interests for as little as $50. Commercial real estate follows similar patterns at larger scale—a tokenized offering for a Manhattan office building recently raised $500 million from over 3,000 investors, each receiving blockchain-based ownership tokens. The efficiency gains are substantial: what once required armies of lawyers and weeks of paperwork now settles in minutes.

Fixed income markets are seeing equally significant innovation. Franklin Templeton's tokenized money market fund holds over $400 million in assets, providing investors with blockchain-based shares that can be transferred peer-to-peer without traditional settlement infrastructure. The World Bank has issued blockchain-based bonds, while corporate issuers increasingly experiment with digital-native debt securities. These instruments combine the reliability of traditional fixed income with the operational efficiency of blockchain rails.

Regulatory clarity has been crucial to this institutional adoption. The SEC's evolving guidance on digital securities, combined with clear frameworks in jurisdictions like Singapore, Switzerland, and the UAE, has given traditional financial institutions confidence to proceed. The distinction between utility tokens and security tokens has crystallized, with tokenized securities falling clearly under existing securities regulations. This regulatory integration, rather than disruption, has proven essential for institutional participation.

Challenges remain substantial, however. Liquidity in secondary markets for tokenized assets remains thin compared to traditional exchanges. The promise of 24/7 global trading means little if counterparties are scarce. Custody solutions, while improving, still lack the institutional-grade infrastructure that traditional assets enjoy. And the technology itself continues evolving—the question of which blockchain standard will dominate for securities remains unsettled, creating interoperability concerns for long-term investors.

For investors evaluating tokenized opportunities, several considerations apply. The underlying asset fundamentals matter at least as much as the tokenization wrapper—a poorly structured real estate deal doesn't become attractive simply because it's on a blockchain. Fee structures deserve scrutiny, as some platforms extract substantial value through transaction fees, management fees, and other charges that can erode returns. And liquidity assumptions should be realistic: while tokens can technically trade continuously, actual trading volumes may not support large positions without significant price impact. Tokenization offers genuine advantages for certain asset classes and investor profiles, but it remains a means rather than an end—a tool that must be evaluated in context rather than embraced uncritically.
