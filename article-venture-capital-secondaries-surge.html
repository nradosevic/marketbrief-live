Venture Capital Secondaries: The $150 Billion Liquidity Opportunity

By Michael Okonjo • March 10, 2026 • 7 min read

The venture capital secondary market has exploded from a niche corner of private markets into a $150 billion opportunity, fundamentally reshaping how startup equity changes hands. Driven by an extended IPO drought, employee liquidity needs, and institutional portfolio rebalancing, secondary transactions now represent a critical component of the venture ecosystem. For buyers, sellers, and the startups caught in between, understanding this rapidly evolving market is essential.

The scale of the opportunity reflects unprecedented conditions. Venture capital firms deployed over $500 billion globally between 2020 and 2022, creating a massive overhang of unrealized positions. The subsequent collapse in public market valuations and near-closure of the IPO window left limited partners without the distributions they'd planned for and employees holding illiquid equity compensation. Secondary buyers—ranging from specialized funds to family offices and sovereign wealth funds—have stepped in to provide liquidity at discounted valuations.

Pricing in the secondary market has evolved considerably. In 2022-2023, discounts of 50-70% to last funding round valuations were common for all but the highest-quality companies. Those discounts have moderated as buyers have gained confidence in valuations and competition for quality assets has intensified. Today, top-tier companies trade at 15-30% discounts, while second-tier names remain at steeper markdowns. The pricing dispersion reflects a bifurcated market where quality commands premiums while struggling companies face liquidity deserts.

For limited partners in venture funds, secondaries offer portfolio management flexibility previously unavailable. Endowments and pension funds overallocated to venture during the boom years can now reduce exposure without waiting for distributions. Family offices can monetize positions in funds with extended holding periods. The trade-off involves accepting discounts that may prove disadvantageous if companies eventually exit at higher valuations—but for many LPs, the certainty and rebalancing benefits outweigh the potential upside foregone.

Employee liquidity has emerged as a particularly active segment. Startup employees, many of whom joined companies expecting IPOs within 3-5 years, now face extended timelines with no clear exit visibility. Tender offers organized by companies allow employees to sell portions of their holdings, though these programs are often limited in size and frequency. Direct secondary sales to specialized buyers provide an alternative, albeit typically at larger discounts and with company approval requirements. The employee secondary market has professionalized rapidly, with platforms matching sellers and buyers while ensuring proper documentation and compliance.

Companies themselves have complicated relationships with secondary activity. On one hand, secondaries provide employee retention benefits by offering liquidity pathways. On the other, trades at steep discounts can establish uncomfortable valuation marks and create governance complications as new shareholders enter the cap table. Many companies have implemented right of first refusal provisions and transfer restrictions that moderate secondary activity while preserving some employee flexibility. The balance between liquidity and control remains company-specific.

Looking ahead, the secondary market is likely to remain elevated even if IPO activity recovers. The venture industry's expanded scale means that secondaries will remain an important liquidity mechanism for years to come. Buyers who develop expertise in valuation, due diligence, and negotiation will continue finding opportunities. Sellers will benefit from increased competition among buyers. And the venture ecosystem will continue adapting to a world where the secondary market is not an afterthought but a fundamental component of the value chain.
