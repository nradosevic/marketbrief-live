Currency Hedging in Volatile Markets: A Practical Framework

By Fatima Al-Hassan • March 2, 2026 • 7 min read

Currency movements can significantly impact returns on international investments, sometimes overwhelming the underlying asset performance entirely. A U.S. investor who captured strong equity returns in Japan or Europe during recent years may have seen those gains diminished—or even erased—by dollar strength against the yen and euro. Understanding when and how to hedge currency exposure has become an essential skill for globally diversified investors navigating increasingly volatile foreign exchange markets.

The decision to hedge begins with understanding exposure. Currency risk arises whenever investments are denominated in foreign currencies—international stocks and bonds, real estate, private equity holdings abroad. The exposure compounds: a European equity investment carries both the underlying stock market risk and the euro/dollar exchange rate risk. These two sources of volatility can reinforce or offset each other, but over longer periods, currency movements often prove unpredictable and can dominate returns.

Arguments against hedging focus on cost and long-term neutrality. Currency hedges typically require rolling forward contracts or options, creating ongoing transaction costs and potential tracking error. Over very long horizons, some research suggests currencies tend toward purchasing power parity, meaning hedging costs may be incurred without improving expected returns. Hedging also eliminates the potential benefit of favorable currency moves—protecting against yen weakness means forgoing gains from yen strength.

Arguments for hedging emphasize volatility reduction and risk management. For investors whose spending needs are in dollars, foreign currency exposure represents uncompensated risk—volatility without commensurate return. Hedging can reduce portfolio standard deviation by 2-3 percentage points for typical international allocations, a meaningful improvement in risk-adjusted returns. During market stress, correlations between equities and currencies often shift unpredictably, making unhedged positions harder to manage.

A practical framework acknowledges that neither full hedging nor no hedging represents an optimal policy for all investors and all market conditions. Strategic hedging ratios should reflect investor circumstances: those with foreign income or spending needs might hedge less, while those with purely domestic liabilities might hedge more. Tactical adjustments around strategic targets can add value when currency valuations reach extremes or when interest rate differentials make hedging unusually cheap or expensive.

Implementation details matter considerably. Forward contracts provide the most direct hedge but require active management as contracts roll. Currency-hedged ETFs handle this complexity but may track imperfectly and add expense ratios. Options offer asymmetric protection—limiting downside while preserving upside—but at premium costs that can erode returns over time. The choice depends on portfolio size, sophistication, and specific risk tolerances.

The current environment presents particular challenges. Interest rate differentials between the U.S. and other developed markets have made hedging costs unusually high for dollar-based investors hedging euro or yen exposure. These costs can exceed 3-4% annually, creating a meaningful drag on hedged returns. Some investors have responded by accepting more currency risk; others view the hedging cost as worthwhile insurance given elevated volatility. There is no universally correct answer—only a framework for making thoughtful decisions aligned with individual circumstances and market conditions.
